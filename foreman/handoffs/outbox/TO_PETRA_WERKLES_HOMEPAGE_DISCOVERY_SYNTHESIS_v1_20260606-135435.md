Top 3 Homepage Problems

Hero message is doing too much. If it's trying to explain what Werkles is while also converting a visitor, it's failing at both. First-time visitors need one clear answer to "what happens when I sign up?" before anything else.
The crucible gate has no preview. Asking someone to commit to a process (account creation, waitlist, form) before they've seen any artifact of the work creates friction that kills curiosity-driven signups. The gate needs a taste on the near side of it.
Visual hierarchy isn't earning the scroll. If the fold doesn't create an unanswered question, visitors won't move. A hero that explains too completely gives people permission to leave satisfied.

Top 3 Homepage Opportunities

Show one real output. A single compelling example of what Werkles produces — anonymized, stylized, whatever — does more conversion work than any headline. Make the artifact the argument.
Name the "before" state explicitly. The people you want feel a specific frustration right now. If the hero names that frustration precisely, they self-select in. Specificity beats aspiration every time.
The signup flow can be a personality moment. Most signup flows are dead air. Werkles has a chance to make the onboarding questions feel like the product has already started — that first interaction sets the tone for everything downstream.

Recommended Hero Message

"You already know what the problem is. Werkles is what you do about it."

Lean, confrontational, assumes a smart visitor. Pairs with a single output artifact below it that shows — not tells — what "doing something about it" looks like.

One Thing Not to Change

Whatever is making people lean forward right now — the name, a visual, a specific line of copy that's already earning attention. Before optimizing anything, find the one element that's already working and protect it. Redesigns most often fail by fixing things that weren't broken while leaving the real problems untouched.

Top 3 Homepage Problems

The "Everything for Everyone" Trap: The current layout tries to speak to every possible audience segment simultaneously. By failing to pick a clear primary beachhead user, the messaging becomes diluted, forcing visitors to dig to figure out if the platform is actually built for them.

Feature-First vs. Value-First: The narrative leans heavily on how Werkles works (the mechanics, the tools, the ecosystem) rather than why it matters. It describes a utility instead of defining a new, undeniable category.

Weak Alternatative Differentiation: The positioning doesn't sharply draw the line between Werkles and legacy alternatives. Without a clear "us vs. them" narrative arc, prospects will naturally bucket Werkles into an existing, crowded software category where it doesn't belong.

Top 3 Homepage Opportunities

Own the Category Narrative: Frame Werkles not just as a tool, but as the definitive operating system for a specific, emerging way of working. Define the "Before" (fragmented, frustrating) and the "After" (seamless, empowered) to establish a high-stakes story arc.

The "Full Crew" Social Proof: Lean heavily into the collective power of the community or ecosystem. Showing the momentum of the "Full Crew" gives individual users the psychological safety of joining a movement rather than adopting an unproven tool.

A Radical, Outcome-Driven Hero Section: Rebuild the fold to immediately call out the exact pain point Werkles eliminates. A bold, polarizing statement will instantly filter in high-intent users and weed out looky-loos.

One Recommended Hero Message

The chaos ends where your crew begins.
Stop hacking together fragile workflows. Werkles gives your entire team the unified ecosystem to build, scale, and own your collective momentum—all in one place.

One Thing Not to Change

The Underlying Authenticity: Do not sanitize the voice. The "Full Crew" energy and the distinct, slightly unconventional personality woven into the brand's DNA are its greatest defense against generic corporate competitors. Keep the grit; just sharpen the focus.
