## 1. Overall Visual Diagnosis

### Why the current imagery direction feels wrong

The fantasy/foundry direction failed because it answered the wrong question. The brief implies transformation and formation of real human potential — operators, builders, connectors assembling real ventures. The imagery answered: *what if this were a fantasy RPG where people forge magical identities?*

That inversion produced glowing portals, forge hammers, concept-art creatures, steampunk aesthetic, giant owl deities, and game-UI chrome. None of those things exist in the world Werkles users actually inhabit. They inhabit desks, laptops, coffee shops, side projects, and underpaid jobs they are trying to escape. The imagery told them they were in the wrong genre.

Secondary failure: correction attempts swung toward stock-photo realism — smiling professionals, construction crews, diversity handshakes. That direction is equally wrong because it has no specificity, no strangeness, no gravity. It says "LinkedIn Premium" rather than "something I've never seen before."

The site has also drifted toward making *the founder's story* the hero, rather than making the *user's story* the hero. Cinematic fog, dramatic lighting, and conceptual abstraction all center Werkles-as-grand-vision rather than user-as-protagonist.

### What must be preserved

- **W+erkles logo/lockup.** It is clean, distinctive, and self-aware. Keep it.
- **Iron Palette as interface language.** The dark, structured, slightly industrial interface tone is correct for the audience. Werkles is not a pastel productivity tool.
- **The underlying insight that people are not trapped.** This is the emotional core and every visual decision should serve it.
- **Squibb's existence.** The owl guide is a good idea. Its current execution needs refinement, not elimination.
- **Bellows as a distinct named space.** The learning area having its own identity is correct.

### What must stop immediately

- All forge/foundry/anvil metaphors in public-facing imagery.
- Glowing energy portals, arcane circles, mystical fire.
- Giant supernatural owl imagery.
- Steampunk RPG set design.
- Any image that looks like it was generated as a game cutscene.
- Cinematic fog as a substitute for compositional clarity.
- Dramatic founder-figure silhouettes.
- Construction crews as a default stand-in for "building."
- Corporate diversity stock photos.
- Real estate agent smiles.
- Luxury hustle imagery (sports cars, private jets, gold anything).
- Guru fog (backlit figures, follower-crowd stagings).

## 2. Core Visual Recommendation

### Is "transformation + formation" a viable direction?

**Yes. Conditionally.**

It is viable if — and only if — it is expressed through specificity rather than metaphor. The failure mode of both the fantasy direction and the stock-photo direction is that they reached for symbols *about* transformation rather than depicting the actual moment of it.

Transformation imagery that works is:
- A person looking at two different versions of their profile and choosing one.
- A set of cards that weren't touching, now snapped into position.
- A dossier that was sparse, now populated with collaborators.
- An interface state that says "incomplete" and then says "formed."

Transformation imagery that fails is:
- A glowing chrysalis.
- A person emerging from smoke.
- A hand reaching toward a glowing gear.
- Before/after stock photos of sad office worker → confident entrepreneur.

The test is: **could this image exist in real life, shot on a real camera, or drawn by an illustrator who works for a serious publication?** If yes, it may work. If it requires AI hallucination to exist, it probably will not.

### How to express it without becoming cheesy, cartoonish, AI-sloppy, or fake cinematic

The visual language should feel like it was made by a smart, slightly eccentric design studio that reads *Monocle* and *Works That Work*, references industrial process photography, and has a deep appreciation for mid-century information design. Not a tech startup. Not a motivational poster. Not a gaming studio.

**Guiding aesthetic poles:**
- Criterion Collection cover design (spare, typographic, purposeful)
- Pentagram identity work for civic or educational institutions
- *Wired* long-form editorial photography from the early 2000s, before everything became hyperreal
- Systems diagrams drawn with human weight — Eames-era information graphics
- Muji product photography: things that have been deliberately placed, not randomly posed

**The emotional register is:** quiet confidence, unusual precision, a sense that things are being assembled rather than performed.

## 3. Visual System

The recommended mix of visual elements for Werkles:

**Human portraits** — Used sparingly. Members, not actors. When a human appears, they are doing something specific and real. No poses. No eye contact with the camera unless intentional. Cropping and framing should feel editorial, not corporate.

**Profile/member cards** — The primary visual unit of the product. These are design-rich and should carry visual weight. A well-designed card IS the transformation artifact. Cards show name, lane, status, and formation state. Cards can be the hero image on many pages.

**Lane cards** — Each lane (Spark, Operator, Backer, Connector, Builder, Worker) gets a distinct visual identity expressed through card design, not through a character or mascot. Color coding within Iron Palette. Minimal iconography. No fantasy archetypes.

**Pairing/formation visuals** — Show two or more profile cards in proximity, with a visual grammar that distinguishes incomplete from complete. Think: dossier folders, puzzle-lock states, connecting lines drawn with precision. Not a dating app. Not a LinkedIn connection animation.

**Artifact/document imagery** — Real-looking proposals, briefs, plans, and contracts, depicted as objects with weight. Not floating paper in light. Documents that have been handled. This family of imagery reinforces that Werkles produces real-world outputs.

**Environment/background imagery** — Functional spaces: real workshops, print studios, independent offices, kitchen tables covered in notes, warehouses with activity in them. Not beauty shots of these spaces. Working shots. Spaces that have been used. No empty corporate lobbies.

**Motion/animation** — Limited. Card flip for lane selection. Snap/lock animation for formation. Progress states filling in. Squibb's idle loop. Nothing that runs without user interaction except a very subtle idle state in the hero.

**Squibb/mascot usage** — See Section 8. Small. Contextual. Useful. Not decorative.

## 4. Human Imagery Rules

### When humans should appear

- Profile cards and member representations.
- Testimonial or case study sections.
- Bellows instructor or guide representations.
- Any section specifically about a person's story or lane.

### How humans should appear

- **Candid or semi-candid.** Natural light where possible. Doing something, not posing.
- **Specific props and environments.** A person at a specific workbench, in a specific kind of office, in front of a specific kind of project. The specificity is what makes them feel real.
- **Editorial framing.** Portrait orientation with negative space. Slightly unexpected crop. Not centered and smiling.
- **Consistent color grading** that aligns with Iron Palette — slight desaturation, cool-warm balance that reads as intentional, not Instagram.
- **Diverse without being a diversity showcase.** People should appear to have been chosen because they are interesting, not because they represent a demographic slot.

### When humans should not appear

- Hero sections where the product or concept is the hero.
- Background textures or decorative fills.
- Anything that requires them to perform an emotion rather than inhabit a moment.
- Formation diagrams (use cards, not faces).

### How to avoid banking/stock-photo people

The test is: **does this person look like they are being photographed, or does this person look like they were caught being themselves?** Stock photos fail the second test. Commission or source photography where the subject has a reason to exist in the frame beyond being a model. If sourcing stock, look for documentary or editorial stock libraries (Offset, Stocksy editorial collections) not the big-box providers.

### How to avoid construction-only framing

"Building" and "making" are not limited to physical construction. A Werkle can be built by a strategist at a laptop as much as a fabricator at a bench. The visual vocabulary of making should span: notebooks, whiteboards, code on screens, physical prototypes, meetings that look like real arguments rather than corporate presentations. Weight the imagery toward *thinking* and *deciding* as much as *doing*.

### How to make people feel real without staged movie scenes

- Remove the lighting setup. Real light, not three-point studio.
- Remove the wardrobe coordination. People dress like themselves.
- Remove the scripted emotion. If someone is smiling, something just happened that was actually funny.
- Add specificity in the background. You can tell what kind of work happens here.
- Add evidence of time. A coffee cup, a marked-up document, a half-finished prototype.

## 5. Transformation Grammar

### The problem to solve visually

The transformation Werkles offers is not physical or magical. It is a shift in self-definition and social legibility. A person who was "just a worker" becomes legible as an Operator or a Connector. That is not a dramatic metamorphosis. It is a label change with enormous real-world consequences.

The visual grammar should match the actual experience: a threshold crossed, a choice made, a new configuration that holds.

### Recommended expressions

**Card state changes** — A profile card in an undeclared state looks spare: name, location, a faint lane outline. After lane selection, the card fills with color coding, role title, skills tags, and formation readiness indicator. The before/after is built into the product interaction, not illustrated separately.

**Labels as transformation artifacts** — "Operator" is a powerful word. It should appear in the UI with visual weight that makes the user feel something when they choose it. Typographic treatment matters here. The lane label should feel like it has been stamped, not typed.

**Before/after profile metadata** — Consider an onboarding interaction where the user fills in their current situation (job title, what they do now) and then selects their lane. The card shows both: *currently* and *lane chosen.* The tension between those two things is the transformation. Show the gap honestly.

**Props and artifacts** — A partially filled dossier next to a completed one. A blank card next to a formed Werkle card. The contrast does not need to be dramatic.

**Onboarding interaction** — The moment of lane selection is the transformation moment. It should have appropriate visual gravity: a pause, a confirmation animation, a card that settles into its final state. Not a fireworks explosion. More like a lock clicking into place.

### What to avoid

- Literal before/after photos of a person looking different.
- Body transformations, wardrobe changes, physical metamorphosis.
- Glowing moments, particle effects, magical reveals.
- Superhero origin framing.
- Any video that shows a person *becoming* something in real time.

## 6. Formation Grammar

### The problem to solve visually

A Werkle is not a person. A Werkle is an assembly. The visual challenge is showing incompleteness without being depressing, and completion without being triumphant kitsch.

The grammar for this should feel like precision fitting: parts that were separate, now aligned. Not puzzle pieces (too casual), not circuit boards (too technical), not handshakes (too corporate).

**Recommended visual vocabulary:**

**Profile card arrays** — The core formation visual is a set of profile cards arranged in a grid or row. Cards for slots not yet filled have a visible absence state: a ghost outline, a lane indicator without a face or name. As a Werkle assembles, the ghost slots fill. The final state has all slots occupied and a Werkle formation indicator activates.

**Dossier system** — A Werkle, when formed, has a dossier: the combined cards, the project brief, the formation date, the roles. This dossier is an artifact with visual weight. It looks like something you would keep. Not a dashboard. An object.

**Diagrammatic joints** — For formation diagrams (how roles connect, what a Werkle needs), use spare, line-based diagramming. Thin lines connecting card-nodes. Not org chart boxes. More like a circuit schematic that has been drawn by hand.

**Locked-piece visual** — When a formation completes, a visual state: all cards slide into precise alignment, a border or frame activates around the assembly, and a "Werkle formed" label appears. This should feel like a filing cabinet drawer closing smoothly, not a slot machine hitting.

**Progress states** — Incomplete: 2 of 5 roles filled. Partially complete: 4 of 5. Formed: 5 of 5 + formation date. Each state is a distinct card/dossier visual, not just a progress bar.

**Solo → pair → crew** — Show the scale of formation: one person with a blank formation space, two cards connected with a single line, a full Werkle with all roles. These are states in the product, not separate pages.

### What to avoid

- Puzzle piece iconography.
- Mechanical gear interlocking.
- Jigsaw metaphors.
- "Team assembly" corporate illustrations.
- Anything that looks like a fantasy party (tank, healer, DPS).

## 7. Page-by-Page Guidance

### Homepage

The hero should answer one question immediately: *What is Werkles for?* The answer is not a cinematic declaration. It is a concrete provocation.

Recommended treatment: A single, well-designed profile card — partially filled, with a lane selected but formation incomplete — against a dark, textured but non-decorative background. Headline typeset with weight. No people in the hero unless it is a real person whose story is being told. The card does the work.

Below the hero: a short formation sequence — three states of a Werkle assembling. Cards, not metaphors. Spare copy.

No fog. No portals. No full-bleed dramatic photography unless it is exceptional and specific.

### Lanes Section

Each lane gets a card. The card is the visual. Lane name, brief definition, a sparse set of attributes, a color indicator within Iron Palette.

Adjacent to each lane card: one real example of a person who chose this lane, shown in the editorial portrait style above. Not a stock model performing ambition. A person who looks like they actually did the thing.

No lane should have fantasy art, iconography of archetypes (the wizard, the knight, the rogue), or motivational poster copy.

### Matching / Formation Section

The formation diagram is the hero of this section. Show the slots. Show the vacancy. Show what fills it. Walk through a complete formation visually using the profile card + dossier grammar.

A secondary element: a real brief from a Werkle formation, lightly anonymized, shown as a document artifact. Grounds the concept in reality.

### Bellows

Bellows has its own visual sub-language, slightly warmer than the main site. It is a learning space, not a product catalog. Imagery here can lean toward workbench / studio / workshop environments: things being made, understood, and practiced. Squibb has more presence in Bellows than elsewhere.

Typography in Bellows should feel slightly more editorial — longer reading line, more whitespace, hierarchy that supports sustained reading rather than scanning.

### Crucible / Proof

This section needs evidence. The visual grammar here is dossier and artifact: real project outcomes, real formation records, real documentation of what a Werkle built. If these don't exist yet, simulate them with high craft — they must look real enough to be believed.

No testimonial headshots with a quote and five stars. If a person is cited, they get a full card treatment.

### Profile / Member Cards

This is Werkles' primary visual unit and it deserves the most design investment. The card should be beautiful and functional. It carries: lane color, role label, formation status, skills, current availability or project state.

Distinguish clearly between: a card that has not yet chosen a lane, a card with a lane chosen but not yet in a formation, a card actively in a Werkle, and a card with a completed Werkle in its history. These states are the transformation story. Do not flatten them into one design.

### Pricing / Membership

This section should be the least visually elaborate on the site. Clean, clear, no decorative imagery. The product understanding built by every other page does the persuasive work. Pricing pages that are overstyled look like they are hiding something.

### Dashboard

The dashboard is Iron Palette at its most functional. Information-dense, clear hierarchy, no decorative flourish. The only imagery is cards — the user's own card, their formation members' cards, any active Werkle dossiers. Squibb may appear here contextually when there is something actionable to flag.

## 8. Squibb Rules

**Where Squibb belongs:**
- Onboarding flows, at specific decision points.
- Bellows, as a learning guide.
- Dashboard, only when there is a concrete action to suggest.
- Empty states, to prevent them from feeling abandoned.

**Where Squibb does not belong:**
- The homepage hero.
- Marketing or acquisition pages.
- Pricing.
- Any section that needs to feel serious and credible on first impression.
- Decorative backgrounds, pattern tiles, or repeated as an emblem.

**How large Squibb should be:**
Small. An owl that sits in the corner of a UI state. Not a character that takes up a column. When Squibb has something to say, the interface gives Squibb a small speech element. When Squibb is silent, Squibb is barely visible. Think: the favicon, not the billboard.

**When Squibb speaks:**
- At a real decision point with a useful thing to say.
- To surface a specific next action ("You have two open formation slots").
- To explain a concept in Bellows when the user is stuck.
- At a milestone ("Your Werkle is formed.").

**When Squibb stays out:**
- When the user is in flow and does not need interruption.
- When the information on screen is already clear.
- When Squibb would have nothing useful to add.
- On pages where a mascot undercuts credibility.

**How to keep Squibb from becoming Clippy:**
Squibb speaks in short, specific, useful sentences. Squibb never says "It looks like you're trying to—". Squibb never volunteers opinion on things the user did not ask about. Squibb does not animate constantly. Squibb's idle state is still. Squibb's appearance should feel like a small, useful creature that respects your time, not a branded character demanding attention.

Squibb's visual design should be refined enough that it reads as illustration quality, not cartoon clip art. A few frames of movement, well-drawn, is worth more than a full animated loop that runs every time the page loads.

## 9. What to Stop Using

The following image categories are not permitted in public-facing Werkles contexts:

- **Fantasy foundry imagery:** forges, anvils, hammers, glowing metal, smithing.
- **Game UI aesthetics:** health bars in non-game contexts, XP meters as decorative elements, quest log stylings.
- **Glowing portals:** arcane circles, dimensional gateways, energy vortices.
- **Steampunk RPG scenes:** gears as decoration, brass pipes, Victorian-fantasy sets.
- **Giant supernatural owl scenes:** Squibb is small. Giant mystical owl imagery is not Squibb.
- **Generic construction crews as default:** hard hats and high-vis as the primary metaphor for "building."
- **Corporate handshake photography:** two people shaking hands in front of a window, any variant.
- **Real estate agent smiles:** direct eye contact, power suit, aspirational neutral background.
- **Luxury hustle imagery:** sports cars, private jets, penthouse offices, watches, gold.
- **Guru fog:** backlit stage figures, crowds reaching upward, motivational silhouettes.
- **AI art showcases:** images that exist to demonstrate generative AI capability rather than serve the user's story.

## 10. What to Test First

Five visual experiments, in priority order:

**Test 1 — Profile Card Design System (UI-only, static)**  
Location: Internal design file, then Lanes and Profile pages.  
What: Design the full profile card in its four states (undeclared, lane chosen, in formation, formed). This is the foundational visual unit. Everything else builds from it.  
Asset type: UI design (Figma or equivalent). No image generation needed.

**Test 2 — Lane Cards (UI-only, static)**  
Location: Lanes section.  
What: Six lane cards, each with Iron Palette color coding, lane name, and three defining attributes. No character art. No iconography until the card design is settled.  
Asset type: UI design. No image generation needed.

**Test 3 — Formation Sequence (UI, minimal motion)**  
Location: Matching/Formation section, possibly Homepage below-fold.  
What: A three-state diagram of a Werkle assembling. State 1: empty slots with ghost outlines. State 2: partially filled. State 3: formed, with dossier visible. Transition is subtle animation on scroll or tap.  
Asset type: UI design + CSS/SVG animation. No image generation needed.

**Test 4 — One Real Environment Photo (sourced photography, static)**  
Location: Bellows hero or a secondary section on the Homepage.  
What: A single photograph of a real working space — not staged, not corporate, not construction. A studio, a workshop, an unusual office. Test whether a real photo at editorial quality grounds the site better than illustration or AI generation. Source from Unsplash editorial, Stocksy, or Offset. Budget: one good image, evaluated before committing to a photo direction.  
Asset type: Licensed photography.

**Test 5 — Squibb Idle State (illustration, static then looping)**  
Location: Bellows, one onboarding state.  
What: Commission or refine a single standing Squibb illustration at two sizes (32px icon, 80px contextual). Test it in one empty state in Bellows. Evaluate: does it add warmth without adding noise?  
Asset type: Commissioned illustration. Do not generate with Ghost Forge yet.

## 11. Ghost Forge Guidance

**Stop generating immediately:**
- Forge, foundry, anvil, smithing scenes.
- Fantasy landscapes, RPG environments.
- Magical energy effects: glowing orbs, portals, arcane circles.
- Giant owl imagery or supernatural animal characters.
- Steampunk or Victorian-industrial aesthetics.
- Cinematic fog and dramatic backlit silhouettes.
- "Transformation" as a physical metamorphosis (chrysalis, emergence, etc.).
- Game UI chrome (health bars, quest logs) as aesthetic elements.
- Concept art in the style of video game promotional materials.

**May generate later (after Test 1–3 are validated and visual system is locked):**
- Sparse, editorial-quality background textures that complement Iron Palette.
- Abstract formation diagrams that do not lean on fantasy or tech metaphors.
- Squibb expressions (only after a human illustrator has established the base design — Ghost Forge should extend, not originate).
- Environmental textures for Bellows (warm, working-space feeling, not dramatic).

**Never generate:**
- Real human faces or portraits (use photography or commissioned illustration).
- Celebrity likenesses, even stylized.
- Images that parody or reference copyrighted characters.
- Anything designed to look like a video game, regardless of quality.
- Imagery that positions Werkles as a luxury or exclusive product.

**Prompt families to replace current fantasy/foundry prompts:**

Replace fantasy language with:
- "editorial photograph, natural light, working space, [specific activity or object]"
- "information design, spare diagram, industrial precision, mid-century graphic style"
- "profile card, clean interface, Iron Palette, dark background, typographic hierarchy"
- "small owl illustration, minimal line weight, quiet expression, off-white on dark background"
- "document artifact, dossier, handled paper, deliberate composition, editorial still life"

The shift is from *metaphor* to *specificity*. Every prompt should be able to answer: what real thing is this? If it cannot, the image should not be generated.
